## Agentic AI Overview

Agentic AI represents a paradigm shift in artificial intelligence, moving beyond reactive systems to create autonomous entities capable of pursuing defined objectives. Traditional AI, exemplified by large language models (LLMs) responding to individual prompts, operates on a request-response basis. Agentic AI, conversely, embodies goal-directed behavior, perceiving its environment, deliberating on actions, and executing plans to achieve those goals without constant human oversight. 

The core characteristics defining agentic systems are: **autonomy**, the ability to operate independently; **reactivity**, responding dynamically to changes in the environment; **proactivity**, taking the initiative to achieve goals rather than solely reacting to stimuli; and **social ability**, the capacity to interact with other agents (AI or human) to coordinate actions or exchange information. 

Technically, modern agentic AI architectures typically center around an LLM functioning as the central reasoning engine. However, the LLM is not operating in isolation. Crucially, agentic systems are augmented with several key components: **tools**, which provide access to external capabilities (e.g., search engines, APIs, databases); **memory**, enabling the agent to retain and utilize past experiences and information; and **planning modules**, responsible for decomposing complex goals into manageable, sequential steps. This planning often involves techniques like hierarchical planning or tree-of-thoughts prompting.

The execution loop of an agentic system generally follows these stages: Observation (perceiving the environment), Planning (determining the next best action), Action (executing the action using available tools), and Learning (updating memory based on the outcome). This cycle repeats until the goal is achieved or a termination condition is met.

Building robust, production-ready agentic systems requires managing state, ensuring persistence, and incorporating mechanisms for human oversight. Frameworks like **LangGraph** address these challenges by providing a structured environment for constructing stateful, multi-step AI workflows. LangGraph facilitates the creation of agents with long-term memory, allowing them to learn and adapt over time. It also supports human-in-the-loop integration, enabling intervention and correction when necessary, crucial for safety and reliability in real-world applications. This course will focus on leveraging LangGraph to build practical agentic AI solutions, covering topics such as agent configuration, tool integration, memory management, and workflow orchestration.
